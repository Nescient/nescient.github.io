18  Apple of My Eye

I love to eat crispy apples in the fall, especially Honey Crisp apples from Ontario Orchards in Oswego!  Apple pie with honey was one of the first things I tried to make as a new bride.  I think I was inspired by that Judy Collin’s song, “We always cook with honey, to sweeten up the night.” That pie became one of my husband’s favorites.  Yet I had this long-standing aversion to apples.  When we were kids, my sister and I had to pick up all the rotten apples from under our two apple trees in the backyard, put them in bushel baskets, drag them across the road, and dump them in the woods.  It was heavy, yucky work, and we were constantly plagued with wasps.  I know now that doing this disgusting work helped me to be responsible, patient, and able to deal with things like poopy diapers. But it made me a little resentful of apples…and my mom. 

I created this “Apple of My Eye” with the Central Square Lioness Apple Festival in mind.  This is a big-deal three day celebration in our area.  It took great courage to sign up for a table!  Since May I have done a few small craft shows, but this one is BIG.  I was wondering if it was morally OK to be so commercial with my artwork—creating something for a show, but I came to really love doing this Circle of Life. I learned a lot about Heirloom Apples and the work going on to protect these old varieties. One of the “rounds” shows 20 different Heirloom apples—the names are on the back. I loved coloring the sample.  I learned that apples originated in Central Asia and got here via early colonists from Europe. 

It’s fascinating to cut an apple around it’s “equator” and see the way the seeds lie in the flesh—that’s what’s represented in the center.  Apples work in 5’s—5 central chambers hold the seeds and the flowers have 5 petals, so I created this Circle of Life as a kind of 5-pointed star. The seeds are germinating in a triangular chamber that echoes the shape of the seed.  Next to these “seed chambers” toward the center are 5 apples cut “longitudinally.”  I learned that this is the preferred way to cut an apple so the ratio of skin to flesh is low; rather that eating the apple “skin-first.”  Apple seeds are scattered throughout the piece with an entire ring of seeds separating the central portion from the spring blossoms.  The “Pac-Man” apples around the outside represent the top of view the apples adjacent to them.

“Apple of My Eye” is more about achieving a pleasing compositional work than conveying deep subject matter. It is a study in the interplay of circles and triangles; light “fairy-like” lines and strong heavy outlines; sinuous lines versus straight lines; and how to make them complement one another.  This was my first “odd” number circle, and required some new ways to “attack” the circular form.  It was a really fun challenge.

The irony of the piece is that no one bought it at the Apple Fest.  Lots of people commented on the design favorably, in fact quite effusively.  The vendor from the local orchard enjoyed naming the heirloom apples in the circle.  People pointed at it, talked about it, took pictures of it; but no one bought it.  Hmmm.
