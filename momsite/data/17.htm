17   Here Be Dragons

This Circle took on a life of its own—growing more and more complicated as I drew!  I think my love affair with dragons began with “Puff the Magic Dragon” echoing in my ears thorough my elementary school years; and then reading The Hobbit back when I was in 7th grade (back when The Lord of the Rings was just a cult classic).  My older sister, Melanie, gave me three books for Christmas that changed my life as a reader—The Hobbit, Around the World in 80 Days, and The Mouse That Roared.  In elementary school I gobbled up all of the “Blue Biographies”—Childhood of Famous Americans in the library (often reading them on trips in the rear-facing bench seat, in the back of our station wagon, sucking up the exhaust fumes, with no seatbelt), and now I wanted to read more sophisticated “grown-up” books in junior high; and my sister set me off on a great adventure!

I never realized that dragons came in so many shapes and forms until I started researching for this project—rooster-like dragons, canine dragons, serpentine dragons, lizard-like dragons, dragons throughout history and in myriad cultures. This Circle of Life was so much fun to dig into and draw!  I want to add that I took some inspiration from Ron at Syracuse Blueprint (my hero at scanning and printing dilemmas), who has a dragon for his screen saver, often wears dragon t-shirts, and I discovered upon completion of this Circle of Life that he even has a dragon tattoo. 

So, on to the description: 

·        The center of the design is inspired by a dragon head created by my son, Orion, while he was in high school, for a large articulated clay slab piece.

·        Four sinuous, two-armed/two-winged dragons surround the center. 

·        In between these images are four German eagles/dragons.  These rigid black creatures have such a sinister look that reflects the Germany of WWII to me—someone said they looked like Nazgul, which is quite appropriate. 

·        As the next layer overlaps, you’ll see a few dragons from the 15th and 16th centuries.  The two opposing dragons with the little short wings were created in Germany in the 1500’s.  This five hundred year old dragon’s name is Draco—which connects him to the Harry Potter stories (ooh, we hate you, Draco Malfoy!). JK Rowling’s world is richly inhabited by dragons.  You’ll also see a couple of smiling story-book dragons in this round.

·        I had to include an image from My Father’s Dragon in the next round.  I joyfully read these stories to our own four kids and also to my second grade students when I was teaching school.  They are charming books, with a cool introduction to map reading.  There is also a curious furry “sea horse” kind of dragon from a Medieval manuscript in this circle.  

·        As you go around the next circle, you’ll see a curious dog-dragon (from the 1800’s), a riff on the Welsh dragon, and a couple of huge Chinese dragon heads.  Apparently Chinese dragons are more whimsical than European dragons.  The extremely spiky, sinuous dragon sneaking through the design is based on a sculpture in a Chinese restaurant.  

·        The proud dragon at the top is from a 19th century engraving.  He is flanked by two dragon heads that were inspired by the artwork of Trina Schart Hyman (one of my absolute favorite illustrators) in Saint George and the Dragon.  If you look at the bottom of this Circle, you’ll see my interpretation of Smaug on his treasure (think back to 7th grade), flanked by two Chinese dragons--these are based on an actual Chinese postage stamp known as the “scariest” stamp in the world. 

·        The background textures (which I became completely obsessive about and REALLY needed to use my artist goggles--thank you Melissa and Orion Roeder) represent dragons’ skin and scales.  The flames (included at the suggestion of my granddaughter Hannah) help tie the last layers together and give it that HOT feeling.

 

I was praying through Psalm 18 one morning during the time I was working on Here Be Dragons and was super excited to see God portrayed as a dragon in King David’s stunning poetry (this is from the Living Bible translation): 

6 In my distress I screamed to the Lord for his help. And he heard me from heaven; my cry reached his ears. 7 Then the earth rocked and reeled, and mountains shook and trembled. How they quaked! For he was angry. 8 Fierce flames leaped from his mouth, setting fire to the earth; smoke blew from his nostrils. 9 He bent the heavens down and came to my defense; thick darkness was beneath his feet. 10 Mounted on a mighty angel, he sped swiftly to my aid with wings of wind. 11 He enshrouded himself with darkness, veiling his approach with dense clouds dark as murky waters. 12 Suddenly the brilliance of his presence broke through the clouds with lightning and a mighty storm of hail.

13 The Lord thundered in the heavens; the God above all gods has spoken—oh, the hailstones; oh, the fire! 14 He flashed his fearful arrows of lightning and routed all my enemies. See how they run! 15 Then at your command, O Lord, the sea receded from the shore. At the blast of your breath the depths were laid bare.

16 He reached down from heaven and took me and drew me out of my great trials. He rescued me from deep waters. 17 He delivered me from my strong enemy, from those who hated me—I who was helpless in their hands.

 

Wow, what a delivery!

What an inspiration for my drawing!
